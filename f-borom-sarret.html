Writing on Film



Borom Sarret (1963)

Directed by Ousmane Sembène





Packs a lot into twenty minutes, following a Senegalese cab driver (Ly Abdoulay) as he struggles to get paid while observing the brutal hierarchies of post-colonial Dakar. Lots of wonderfully detailed shots, and Sembène draws out the enduring great visual and auditory contrasts between Médina (designated the "native quarter" for the African population by French colonial officials in 1914) and the wealthy modernist Plateau. This was Sembène's first film after a year of studying filmmaking in the Soviet Union (and he was previously an active member of the French Communist Party while living in Paris and Marseill), and Sembène deftly uses the cinematic tools of montage to demonstrate the missed opportunities for class solidarity. Abdolay is consistently forced to protect his own individual interests against those of a disabled beggar and a grieving father, and the most pointed cutting in the film is between him handing over his cash to a flattering Griot at the same time as unnoticed beside him a fellow worker is denied payment — mirroring the protagonist's own humiliating treatment throughout the film.
