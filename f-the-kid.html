Writing on Film



The Kid (1921)

Directed by Charlie Chaplin





Such a moving film. Part of what makes Chaplin’s first feature so touching is how tinged with sadness even the happiest moments are (although a couple of moments are just through-and-through heartbreaking). The happinesses in this film, of which there’s so many, all come from a place of joyful movement - the way the Tramp dances around obstacles, pirouetting in and out of each catastrophe. Of course, the necessity of it all is what lends it tragedy, and The Kid takes the poverty around which its plot takes place very seriously. It’s difficult not to see Chaplin’s own background of extreme poverty reflected in the way the Tramp simply has no alternative but to live with inexhaustible enthusiasm and buoyant naivety (and it’s the insight into the fantastical perspective necessary for his survival that makes the Tramp’s dream sequence fit). There’s endless beauty and happiness in watching the way the Tramp navigates the world, though, and the depth and kindness of the character shine even brighter against such a cruel world.
